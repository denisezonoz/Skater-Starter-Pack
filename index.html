How to Stand & Balance

When you first step on a skateboard:

  1. Place your front foot roughly over the front bolts of the deck.
  2. Slightly bend your knees, keep shoulders relaxed and eyes looking ahead (not at your feet).
  3. Once you’re comfortable, shift your weight gently from heel to toe of your front foot—this helps you feel how the board moves.
  4. Practice staying in your stance for 30 seconds on flat ground, then push off lightly and stop to feel stability.

Pro Tip: A stable stance = less wobble. On your first day, wear helmet + wrist guards to build confidence.

History of Skating

Skateboarding emerged in California during the late 1940s and early 1950s as surfers sought to emulate the feeling of riding waves on land when the ocean was flat. Initially, these creations were rudimentary, often just wooden planks with roller skate wheels attached, leading to the name "sidewalk surfing". This innovative fusion of surf culture and street recreation led to the development of the first commercial skateboards by companies like Hobie and Makaha in the 1960s. Skateboarding's popularity surged, culminating in the first skateboarding competition in 1963 in Hermosa Beach, California. Despite this initial boom, concerns over safety and the limitations of early equipment led to a decline in popularity by 1965.

The sport experienced a significant revival in the 1970s, largely due to two key innovations: the introduction of polyurethane wheels and the emergence of innovative skating styles. Frank Nasworthy's development of polyurethane wheels in 1972 drastically improved board control and paved the way for new tricks and maneuvers. Concurrently, the legendary Zephyr team, or Z-Boys, from Santa Monica, California, revolutionized the sport with their aggressive, surf-inspired style in drained swimming pools, popularizing vertical skateboarding. The invention of the "ollie" by Alan Gelfand in 1978 further expanded the possibilities of skateboarding, allowing riders to launch into the air without grabbing the board.

The late 20th and early 21st centuries saw skateboarding solidify its place as a global sport and cultural phenomenon. The 1980s fostered the growth of street skating, with pioneers like Rodney Mullen developing foundational tricks such as the kickflip and the impossible. Skateboarding's integration into popular culture was amplified through media exposure like the X Games, which debuted in 1995, and popular video game series like Tony Hawk's Pro Skater. This culminated in skateboarding's debut as an Olympic sport at the Tokyo 2020 Summer Olympics (held in 2021), a testament to its evolution from a niche pastime to a globally recognized athletic and artistic expression.

Benefits of Skating

Strengthen the Body

Skateboarding is an excellent full-body workout that strengthens muscles and improves cardiovascular health. While many assume it only works the legs, a skater’s entire body is engaged to maintain balance and perform tricks. Pushing the board builds significant lower body strength, while the core muscles—including the abdominals, obliques, and lower back—are constantly activated to stabilize the body, especially on uneven terrain. Furthermore, the quick, explosive movements required for tricks provide a fantastic cardio workout, raising the heart rate and improving oxygen flow. Even casual, flat-ground cruising burns a significant number of calories and provides a low-impact alternative to running.

Strengthen the Mind

Beyond physical exercise, skateboarding significantly enhances mental and emotional resilience. The process of learning a new trick is often marked by repeated failure and falling, which teaches persistence and patience. Skateboarders learn to brush themselves off and try again, building mental toughness and a growth mindset. The focus required to concentrate on a trick or navigate a spot can also be meditative, providing an escape from daily stresses and improving concentration. This cycle of challenging oneself, failing, and ultimately succeeding is highly rewarding and boosts overall confidence.

Strengthen the Community

Skateboarding is known for fostering a strong sense of community and social connection. Skateparks and local spots serve as hubs where people from diverse backgrounds meet, bond over a shared passion, and form friendships. This sense of belonging is a key component of mental well-being, combating loneliness and social isolation. Skateboarding is an inclusive activity, often welcoming people regardless of age, race, or gender, with communities built on mutual respect and a shared stoke for the sport. This supportive environment allows skaters to learn from one another and celebrate each other's successes, strengthening social bonds that can last a lifetime.
